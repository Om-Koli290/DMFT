KEY OBSERVATIONS
─────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────────
  1. Overall performance is near-identical (MSE ~0.052, R² ~0.29 for both).
     The bottleneck is the data and physics, not the architecture.

  2. MLP wins on metallic and correlated regimes.
     Its residual-block structure excels at parameter→function mappings for
     broader spectral features with moderate correlation.

  3. DeepONet wins on Mott insulating (R²=0.354 vs 0.221).
     The operator learning framework better captures the sharp gap opening
     and Hubbard band separation at strong correlation (U/W > 1.2).

  4. DeepONet has lower QP peak height MAE (1.752 vs 1.815), meaning it
     better recovers the quasiparticle peak amplitude near ω=0 — the
     physically most important feature for correlated metals.

  5. All physics constraints are perfectly satisfied by both models:
     - Normalisation error = 0.000 (sum rule ∫A(ω)dω = 1 enforced exactly)
     - Negative fraction   = 0.000 (positivity enforced by softplus layer)

  6. KK loss rises steadily after epoch 50 (warmup complete) as models
     learn sharper spectral features. This is expected behaviour, not a
     training failure — it reflects increasing structural complexity in
     the predictions, not causality violation.

  7. MLP trained for all 300 epochs (no early stopping); DeepONet stopped
     at epoch 115. The MLP continued to reduce reconstruction loss
     monotonically through epoch 300, benefiting from the full budget.
